2.Persona (Base)
1. Company Profile
Question: Why does DRG have multiple capital needs?
Bullet points:
DRG is an agribusiness company operating mainly in rubber and related activities.
Its funding needs are not uniform because different business activities require different capital structures.
The company needs short-term funding for liquidity, working capital, and operational continuity.
It also needs long-term funding for plantation investment, strategic projects, and business expansion.
DRG appears to prioritize long-term investments, which increases the need for stable and properly matched capital.
Financial statements suggest pressure from maturity mismatch and working capital stress.
This means DRG cannot treat all financing needs as one general borrowing request.
Key insight:
DRG is a multi-capital-needs company with both short-term survival pressure and long-term investment requirements.
Its capital needs differ by purpose, timeline, and risk.
Therefore, fundraising must be structured by project or funding case, not handled as one generic loan request.
2. Primary Internal User
Question: Who inside DRG would use this platform?
Bullet points:
The platform would mainly be used by internal enterprise stakeholders, not by retail or external users.
Likely users include the CEO, finance team, strategy team, and project owners.
The finance team handles financial records, lender communication, and funding preparation.
The strategy team aligns each funding case with DRG’s long-term direction and capital priorities.
Project owners contribute project-specific data, timelines, and operational requirements.
Senior management decides what information can be shared and which funding path should be prioritized.
Because DRG values strong internal control and transparency, the main users are likely people involved in internal coordination and approval.
Key insight:
The user is not just one person, but an internal decision-making group responsible for coordinating capital access.
Since DRG emphasizes internal control, transparency, and early restructuring, the platform must support collaboration across management, finance, and project teams.
3. Goals
Question: What does DRG want to achieve with the platform?
Bullet points:
Standardize fundraising materials across different projects and financing cases.
Separate each funding need into its own room or structured process.
Control who can access specific information.
Speed up lender and investor responses.
Present the company in a more professional and credible way.
Maintain good relationships with banks and external capital providers.
Support stable cash flow management despite rubber price fluctuations.
Stay focused on core business instead of spreading financial resources too thin.
Improve efficiency and cost control in the capital-raising process.
Key insight:
DRG’s goal is not just to raise money faster.
It wants a disciplined, efficient, and credible fundraising process that supports liquidity stability, lender trust, and long-term strategic focus.
4. Pain Points
Question: What problems does DRG face in the current process?
Bullet points:
Funding documents are fragmented across teams and sources.
Different lenders and investors ask for different types of data.
One fixed file package does not satisfy all counterparties.
Sensitive information is difficult to share in a controlled way.
The company may have to repeatedly explain the same project or financial story to many parties.
Liquidity concerns make financing delays more painful.
Difficulty in obtaining bank loans increases pressure on the team.
Rubber price volatility and market changes make capital planning harder.
Export policy risks and external market uncertainty can affect lender confidence.
Financial patterns such as OCF volatility, working capital stress, and inflexible cost structure may make the company look riskier to funders.
Key insight:
DRG’s main pain point is not only limited access to capital.
It is the combination of fragmented information, repetitive fundraising work, confidentiality risk, and financial uncertainty.
The process is hard to scale, hard to control, and vulnerable to external shocks.

How their pricing model works
Intralinks uses a combination of pricing variables:
Per-page pricing - approximately $0.60 per page uploaded (industry-reported)
Per-user pricing - charges may apply for users who need to create workstreams (not just view)
Storage-based pricing - costs scale with data volume
Project duration - longer deals cost more
Feature tier - AI tools, advanced reporting, and premium support add cost
There's no monthly subscription you can just sign up for. You negotiate a contract.
Estimated cost ranges (based on industry data)
Small deal / startup fundraising round: Annual cost estimate: $10,000 - $30,000 Typical data volume: 1-5 GB Users: 10-50
Mid-market M&A transaction: Annual cost estimate: $50,000 - $200,000 Typical data volume: 10-50 GB Users: 50-200
Large enterprise deal: Annual cost estimate: $200,000+ Typical data volume: 50 GB+ Users: 200+
Per-page pricing example
If you use a per-page pricing model at $0.60/page:
10,000 pages = $6,000
50,000 pages = $30,000
100,000 pages = $60,000
2 million pages (large deal) = $1,200,000
This is why per-page pricing can become unpredictable fast. One large document dump and costs spike.

This platform will give up on the Enterprise Setup Fee in the early-stage to reduce onboarding friction and accelerate ecosystem development. This fee was built to cover the fixed cost of structuring of enterprise data, classification of capital cases, and configuration of access control. As the platform matures, it can be reintroduced as a one-time charge to ensure commitment and cover operational costs. After total of 10000 users or 10000 SMEs projects, this fee will be executed
The next layer would be the main stream of revenue for this platform, Project Room Fee, which will be generated every time corporations create a deal room. On the investor side, the platform introduces an Investor Access Fee, applied when investors request deeper access to project-specific information.. Working capital for operating business

Unlike traditional virtual data room providers that charge based on data volume or number of pages, this model prices based on the deal itself, better reflecting its economic value but not as good as express complexity.
=> The cost of operating project rooms is distributed across both enterprises and investors through a staged access model, where enterprises bear the initial setup cost while investors contribute as they progress deeper into the deal.
A key differentiator is the Success Fee, charged only when a deal reaches a settled milestone, such as committed capital or actual disbursement. This outcome-based approach aligns the platform’s incentives with enterprise success and reduces the cost pressure for the second layer, creating an advantage of fee when compared to other software if the project didn't reach the ideal outcome.

Jobs to Be Done: Là một lăng kính (góc nhìn) để hiểu các động lực cơ bản thúc đẩy quyết định của khách hàng.
Lý thuyết Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)
JTBD là một khung phân tích để hiểu hành vi khách hàng.
Khách hàng không mua sản phẩm dựa trên nhân khẩu học hoặc đặc điểm của họ.
Thay vào đó, họ “thuê” (hire) một sản phẩm hoặc dịch vụ để thực hiện một “công việc” (job) cụ thể — tức là một nhiệm vụ hoặc sự tiến bộ mà họ muốn đạt được để tiến tới một mục tiêu hoặc khát vọng trong những hoàn cảnh cụ thể.
Xây dựng JTBD cần phải chú ý 3 yếu tố
Functional The financial or operational objective to accomplish
Example: “Ensure suppliers are paid on time without increasing short-term borrowing”
Emotional How the decision-maker (e.g., CFO, Finance Director, CEO…) feels when facing business friction
Example: “I want to feel confident that liquidity risks are under control.”
Social How the firm or manager wants to be perceived
Example: “I want to maintain credibility with owners, lenders, and auditors.”
Practical formula
Instead of using the traditional User Story (“As a [user], I want to…”), JTBD uses the Job Story to capture the context of the struggle:
When I [Situation], I want to [Action/Solution], so that I can [Expected Outcome].
Example: “When I am commuting on a long, boring drive, I want something to snack on for a long time, so that I can stay awake and the journey feels less tedious.”
Các bước để làm JTBD
Step 1: Define the Core Job What progress is the customer trying to make? Note: Avoid mentioning your specific product or solution here.
Step 2: Context & Circumstances When does this job occur? Where? With whom? What triggers this need in their daily life?
Step 3: Barriers & Pains What is preventing them from completing the job currently? What are the risks, frustrations, or trade-offs they are forced to make?
Step 4: Current Solutions & Trade-offs How are they solving the problem right now (or which competitor's product are they “hiring”)? What are the shortcomings of that current solution?
Step 5: Desired Outcomes (Success Metrics) What criteria do they use to measure success? (Is it faster? Cheaper? More peace of mind?)
Step 6: The Four Forces of Progress This is a unique element of JTBD that analyzes the “tug-of-war” in the customer's mind:
Push: Frustration with the current solution.
Pull: The attractiveness of the new solution (your product).
Anxiety: The fear or uncertainty of switching to something new.
Habit: The comfort and inertia of the old way of doing things.

2025 shows a large gap between accounting profit and cash generation, analysis indicates a quality-of-earnings warning for DRG:

Profit vs cash decouples (earnings quality down): NPAT rises to VND 197bn (from VND 122bn), yet Net Operating Cash Flow flips to -VND 77bn (from +VND 168bn). Cash conversion deteriorates from 1.38x to -0.39x. This suggests 2025 profit is not cash-backed.

In the cash flow bridge, underlying operating cash capacity was positive, but the swing is dominated by “Other operating payments” of -VND 258bn, which is the single largest driver pushing Operating Cash Flow to -VND 77bn.
This is a year where (1) margins compressed, (2) the cash cycle lengthened, and (3) a large ‘other operating payments’ cash outflow hit, resulting in negative operating cash despite higher profit. Underwriting should treat and discount 2025 NPAT as lower-quality.

2022 → 2024: Stayed ~75 days (inefficiency persistent).
